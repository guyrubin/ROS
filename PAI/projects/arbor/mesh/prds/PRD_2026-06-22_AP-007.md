## Problem Brief

### Persona and age-band

**Primary parent persona:** The skeptical modern parent — typically a first-time parent of a child aged 0–3, or a returning parent entering Arbor for a second child. They are digitally literate, have heard the news about child-data leaks and colonizing kid-AI, and have developed a default posture of distrust toward apps that want information about their child. They download Arbor with real intent (a specific concern, a recommendation from another parent) and almost immediately encounter the moment of truth: the app asks for the child's name, age, and what they are worried about. At that moment, nothing on screen explains who holds this data, what it is used for, or why the app can be trusted with the most private information a parent owns.

**Secondary persona:** The Israeli parent in the viral-first beachhead — less suspicious of data per se, but acutely sensitive to being sold to. Sees a paywall-adjacent product as requiring a credibility signal before they engage. The trust chip functions as that signal in Hebrew/RTL as much as in English.

### Job-to-be-done

"When I am about to enter my child's name and the reason I am worried about them, I need to know who holds this data and what it will be used for — so I can decide whether to trust this product with my child's story."

This is not a legal consent job. The parent is not reading a privacy policy. They need one calm, specific signal that answers three questions in under three seconds: Is this private? Does Arbor actually hold it responsibly? Will this data work against me later (ads, AI training, third parties)?

### Evidence it is real

1. **CIL finding, score 22.** The arbor-evaluator trust lens, cycle 2026-06-22, confirmed the absence: the privacy statement in AvatarCreator.tsx:194 is the app's only trust signal, and it lives behind a paywall feature the new parent never reaches at onboarding. The finding is adversarially verified code evidence, not a hypothesis.

2. **Industry signal.** In 2026, the app-store-review pattern for child apps in Israel and Europe (App Store / Google Play, sourced by arbor-critic-feedback) shows "data safety" as a rising top-3 uninstall trigger across apps in the parenting and education categories. Parents who abandon in the first session increasingly cite not knowing what the app does with their child's information. Arbor's activation cohort has not yet been measured, but the structural gap is a known conversion and retention risk.

3. **Category context.** Arbor's brand strategy (BRAND-STRATEGY.md §4) names trust-as-GTM as a genuine 2026 differentiator: "parent-owned data, parent-named controller, no open kid-companion — in 2026 that IS the differentiator — a headline, not buried compliance text." The product has the substance (append-only, parent-approved, no AI training on child data) but does not surface it at the moment a parent needs to decide.

4. **Structural analogy.** The "Arbor actually knows my kid" value proposition requires the parent to put something in. The moat is the record. If the parent holds back at step one because nothing reassures them, the record never starts, the moat never builds, and the product's entire thesis collapses before the parent sees a single insight. Activation and the longitudinal value proposition are the same problem.

### Why now

This is not a nice-to-have. Wave PM-01 includes the paywall price display (AP-004) and the post-checkout activation sheet (AP-006). Both of those features assume the parent completed onboarding. If the parent does not complete onboarding — because nothing at the hand-over moment gave them a reason to trust the product — then AP-004 and AP-006 have no parent to convert. AP-007 is the gate that the rest of the wave depends on reaching.

## Scope: the smallest valuable slice

### What this is

A single-line trust chip component, added at the first child-data capture step in OnboardingFlow. The chip renders three specific trust facts about Arbor's data model, links to the existing privacy/safety view, and is available in English and Hebrew/RTL. No new policy pages. No new consent flow. No new backend. No new data handling. The work is a component, a token, four i18n strings, and copy.

### What this is not

- A redesign of the onboarding flow.
- A new privacy policy page.
- A GDPR consent gate (that is AP-003/AP-008 territory and is gated).
- A trust badge or certification claim ("ISO-certified," "HIPAA-compliant") — those are claims Arbor has not established and must not make.
- A replacement for the longer privacy text in AvatarCreator — that stays where it is and covers the photo-specific surface.

## UX intent and state definitions

**arbor-ux owns the flow and interaction.** **arbor-design owns the component and token layer.** The following is the product intent that hands off to both.

### Placement

The chip appears at or immediately below the first field that captures child data in OnboardingFlow. Based on the existing code (OnboardingFlow.tsx), this is the step where the parent enters the child's name and age — the first genuine hand-over moment. It should appear on that screen, not on the welcome screen before any data is requested, and not on a later step where the parent is already past the decision.

### Copy (EN)

> "Private by default · parent-approved memory · never used to train AI"

Each phrase answers one of the three parent questions:
- "Private by default" — answers: who can see this?
- "parent-approved memory" — answers: who controls what is stored?
- "never used to train AI" — answers: will this data be used against me?

The chip links to the existing safety/privacy view. The link text is "Learn more" or equivalent — not "Privacy Policy" (legalese register) and not "Safety Promise" (overclaims).

### Copy (HE, for arbor-content Hebrew review)

Working translation for review:

> "פרטי כברירת מחדל · זיכרון מאושר על ידי הורים · לא משמש לאימון בינה מלאכותית"

**Note:** This requires a native Hebrew review pass from arbor-content before ship. The register must match Arbor's Hebrew brand voice — calm, direct, non-legalese. RTL direction must be confirmed in the component.

### Chip mirroring in profile

The same chip, or an equivalent single-line signal, must also appear near the first-capture field in the child profile edit view. A parent who skips setup and goes directly to creating a profile should see the same trust signal. This is a second render of the same component, not a second design task.

### States

| State | What the parent sees | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Default (EN, new onboarding) | Chip renders inline below the name/age field. Three dot-separated phrases. Tappable "Learn more" link. | Primary case |
| Default (HE, RTL) | Same chip, Hebrew text, right-to-left layout. Link text in Hebrew. | arbor-design confirms RTL token behavior |
| Loading (onboarding step transition) | Chip is visible before and through the transition — it must not disappear during a loading state that causes the parent to question whether the app is working | Not a shimmer state; static component, no async dependency |
| Error (onboarding step fails) | Chip remains visible. It is not a form field; it has no error state of its own. The surrounding form shows the error. | Static |
| No chip (citation absent) | Does not apply — the chip is unconditional on this step. It does not depend on a data fetch. | The copy is static; no "show if" condition |
| Chip on profile screen | Same visual; same three phrases; same link. Renders adjacent to the child name or age field. | Reuse of the same component, no variant needed |

### Accessibility

- The chip text must be readable at WCAG 2.1 AA contrast against the onboarding screen background token.
- The "Learn more" link must be keyboard-navigable and have a visible focus state.
- Touch target for "Learn more" must meet Arbor's 44px minimum.
- The chip must not break RTL layout on Hebrew screens.

### Brand voice check (arbor-brand gate)

The chip copy must pass the Arbor Bar test (BRAND-STRATEGY.md §10). Specifically: it must not be legalese; it must speak to the parent as a capable adult; it must be specific to what Arbor actually does (not a generic privacy disclaimer). The three phrases above are designed to be as specific as possible — they describe Arbor's actual data model, not a policy aspiration.

The phrase "never used to train AI" is load-bearing and accurate: Arbor does not use parent-entered child observation data to retrain its language models. This must be confirmed accurate before ship by arbor-safety. If the technical reality changes, this phrase must be removed before the change ships.

## Acceptance criteria

1. A trust chip component is added to OnboardingFlow at the first child-data capture step (name/age/concern), rendering the three approved phrases as a single inline line with a "Learn more" link to the existing safety/privacy view.

2. The chip renders in English and Hebrew. Hebrew rendering is right-to-left, confirmed in a device-frame or preview screenshot at PR review.

3. The same chip component renders in the child profile first-capture view.

4. The chip has no async dependency — it renders immediately on screen load, not after a data fetch or animation.

5. The "Learn more" link routes to the existing safety/privacy view (no new page required; if that view does not exist at a stable path, arbor-safety routes it before this ticket closes).

6. The chip passes WCAG 2.1 AA contrast against the onboarding screen background token.

7. The phrase "never used to train AI" is confirmed technically accurate by arbor-safety before the PR merges.

8. No new clinical, developmental, or effect-size claim is introduced anywhere in this ticket. The chip contains zero developmental framing.

9. The onboarding completion rate is measured before and after ship (see metric below). No regression is acceptable.

10. arbor-brand reviews the English and Hebrew copy for brand voice before merge. Off-voice copy (legalese, generic, overclaimed) is a blocker.

## Success metric and instrumentation

**The one metric:** Onboarding step-completion rate at the child-data capture step — measured as the ratio of parents who advance past the name/age/concern step to parents who reach it.

**Target:** Stable or improved. The null hypothesis is that the chip does not cause parents to pause, reconsider, or abandon. The positive hypothesis is that it increases completion rate by removing a latent objection. Either outcome is acceptable; a regression (drop-off rate increases after the chip ships) is the failure signal that triggers review.

**Secondary signal (qualitative, pre-scale):** In any usability observation or parent interview conducted within 30 days of ship, note whether parents mention data privacy at the onboarding step unprompted. Pre-ship silence on the topic is consistent with the finding that trust is invisible; post-ship references to the chip ("I saw it says private by default") confirm the signal is landing.

**Instrumentation required:**
- A `step_viewed` event on the child-data capture step in OnboardingFlow, if not already firing.
- A `step_completed` event on the same step.
- Both events carry `locale` (EN / HE) so the metric can be split by language.
- A `trust_chip_link_tapped` event on the "Learn more" link, to measure whether parents actively investigate.

**Owner:** arbor-sre logs the events; arbor-acquisition holds the conversion funnel view. The Head of Product reads the metric at 14 days post-ship and writes a learning entry to PRODUCT-BACKLOG.md.

## Risks

| Risk | Severity | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| "never used to train AI" is inaccurate as Arbor's AI capabilities evolve | High — a false trust claim is worse than no trust signal | arbor-safety confirms technical accuracy before merge; the phrase is a named string constant that any future AI-training change must audit |
| Hebrew copy is awkward or reads as machine-translated | Medium — damages brand voice in the primary viral market | Native Hebrew review by arbor-content before ship; treat as a blocker, not polish |
| The chip is mistaken for a compliance badge or certification by parents or regulators | Low — the three phrases are factual descriptions, not certification claims | Do not add a checkmark icon, a "certified" label, or a third-party logo. Chip is text-only with a link |
| The chip causes new questions the onboarding flow does not answer, increasing abandonment | Low | The "Learn more" link routes to a real explanation. If the linked view does not exist or is empty, block the ship |
| RTL layout breaks the chip text or overflows the "Learn more" link target | Low but must test | arbor-design confirms RTL token behavior in preview before PR merge |

## Clinical and safety routing

**No developmental or medical claim in this ticket.** The chip is a data-handling statement, not a developmental framing. No arbor-clinical-lead sign-off is required as a gate.

**One advisory note** (not blocking): the phrase "parent-approved memory" could theoretically be read as implying a clinical review of what is stored. The intended meaning is that only the parent decides what observations go into the record — no AI autonomously writes to the child's memory without parent action. If arbor-clinical-lead reviews this PRD during a council cycle, this phrasing is worth a quick confirm. It is not a claim the clinical board needs to gate.

**arbor-safety confirmation required** (gating): the accuracy of "never used to train AI" must be confirmed against the current production data-handling architecture before the PR merges. This is a single technical check, not a policy review.
